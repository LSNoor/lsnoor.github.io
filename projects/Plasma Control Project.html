Plasma Control Project

2022-2023

Overview

During my work at Dragonfly/Comet, I was part of a team aiming to tackle challenges related to the control of a plasma etching machine. The team included plasma scientists and ML research engineers and the work was conducted in San Jose, CA and Montréal, QC.

We optimized the experimental control of constrained plasmas by combining reinforcement learning with uncertainty-driven active learning using committee and ensemble surrogate disagreement as the acquisition signal alongside Bayesian optimization.

We also developed a physics-informed digital twin simulator to train and validate selections strategies.

While details on this project are under NDA, feel free to contact me for details, I will do my best to answer your question.

Translated abstract

Surrogate Modeling & Active Learning for Industrial Machine Control

Controlling and optimizing production processes is a fundamental challenge in modern industry, particularly in capital-intensive sectors such as microelectronics. This work addresses the development of a surrogate model—a neural network trained via supervised learning—to faithfully reproduce the behavior of a complex industrial machine ("Machine X") treated as a black box. Since operating the machine is costly and constrained, building a reliable simulator enables faster, parallelizable experimentation without physical access to the system.

A key challenge lies in the scarcity of labeled data: Due to the extent of the input space, exhaustive grid-based exploration of the input space is infeasible, and random sampling fails to prioritize the most informative regions. To address this, we employ active learning through Query by Committee (QBC), a model-agnostic method that identifies high-uncertainty regions by measuring prediction disagreement among an ensemble of bootstrap-trained models. We propose and compare three QBC variants: (1) a standard pool-based approach, (2) a query synthesis approach using optimization to converge toward high-variance points, and (3) a Neural Adjoint QBC (NA-QBC) variant that introduces a learned variance estimator prior to optimization.

Experiments conducted on benchmark functions in one and two dimensions show that all three variants yield performance equivalent to or slightly better than random sampling, with a notable ability to detect discontinuities and critical regions of the input space. The methods show particular promise during early iterations, and this theoretical advantage is expected to increase in higher-dimensional settings (>2), which remains to be validated. Additionally, the proposed solution supports automated input transmission to the real machine, eliminating manual adjustments and significantly accelerating the overall data acquisition process.
